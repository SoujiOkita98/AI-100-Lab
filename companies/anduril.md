# Anduril Industries

## Overview

Anduril Industries is a defense technology company building autonomous systems and AI-powered software for national security. Named after the sword in _The Lord of the Rings_, Anduril was founded in 2017 with the thesis that Silicon Valley's software and hardware engineering talent could fundamentally reshape how the U.S. military acquires and deploys technology.

The company's core platform is **Lattice OS**, an AI operating system that fuses data from diverse sensors and autonomous systems into a unified command-and-control interface. On top of Lattice, Anduril builds and sells a growing portfolio of autonomous hardware:

- **Sentry Tower** -- autonomous surveillance towers (original product, deployed on the US-Mexico border)
- **Ghost** -- small autonomous UAS for ISR
- **Altius** -- family of loitering munitions / tube-launched drones
- **Fury** -- autonomous combat aircraft (UCAV), unveiled 2023
- **Dive-LD** -- extra-large autonomous underwater vehicle (acquired via Dive Technologies)
- **Omen** -- hover-to-cruise autonomous air vehicle (unveiled November 2025)
- **Barracuda** -- counter-UAS interceptor
- **Pulsar** -- electronic warfare / counter-drone system
- **Solid rocket motors** -- produced at their Mississippi factory (opened August 2025), with capacity target of 6,000 tactical motors/year by end of 2026

## Team

**Palmer Luckey** (Founder) is the public face of the company. A college dropout who built Oculus VR as a teenager and sold it to Facebook for $2.3B, he pivoted to defense after being pushed out of Facebook in 2017. He met co-founder Trae Stephens at a Founders Fund retreat in 2014, where they bonded over the idea of applying startup culture to defense.

**Brian Schimpf** (CEO) and **Matt Grimm** (COO) are both Cornell engineers who co-founded the university's DARPA Grand Challenge autonomous vehicle team. Both went to Palantir before Anduril.

**Trae Stephens** (Executive Chairman) is also a Partner at Founders Fund. His background spans Georgetown's School of Foreign Service, the US Intelligence Community (computational linguistics for Arabic/Persian), and Palantir's defense/intelligence growth team. He serves on the President's Intelligence Advisory Board.

**Joseph Chen** (Co-Founder) was an early Oculus VR hardware engineer who later enlisted in the Army National Guard as a paratrooper.

The founding team represents a deliberate fusion of two DNA strands: **Palantir** (government/defense software, security clearances, IC relationships) and **Oculus** (consumer hardware at scale, rapid prototyping, Palmer's personal network and capital).

## What makes them interesting

1. **Inverted business model.** Traditional defense primes wait for government RFPs and build to spec on cost-plus contracts (8-10% margins). Anduril self-funds R&D, builds products speculatively, then sells them at firm fixed prices with 40-45% gross margins. This is the core structural innovation.

2. **Speed of iteration.** Anduril operates on Silicon Valley product cycles (months) rather than Pentagon acquisition cycles (years/decades). Their Lattice OS provides a software backbone that lets them integrate new hardware quickly.

3. **Manufacturing ambition.** Arsenal-1, their $1B, 5-million-square-foot autonomous weapons manufacturing facility in Ohio, is scheduled to begin production in July 2026 and will create 4,000+ jobs. Combined with their Mississippi solid rocket motor factory, Anduril is building vertically integrated production capacity at a scale no defense startup has attempted.

4. **Revenue trajectory.** ~$1B in 2024, estimated ~$2.1B in 2025 (100%+ YoY growth). If the growth rate holds, they are on track to rival mid-tier defense primes within a few years.

5. **Contract wins signal institutional acceptance.** The $22B IVAS partnership with Microsoft/Army, $642M Marine Corps counter-drone deal, and participation in major ACAT I programs show that Anduril has crossed from "interesting startup" to "serious prime competitor."

6. **Geopolitical tailwind.** Rising global defense spending, US-China competition, the Ukraine war's demonstration of autonomous systems' battlefield value, and bipartisan support for defense modernization all favor Anduril's positioning.

## Risks and open questions

- **Concentration risk.** Revenue is overwhelmingly US government. Policy shifts, budget sequestration, or procurement scandals could impact growth.
- **IPO timing.** With a $60B target valuation and no public market listing yet, the company faces pressure to either IPO or demonstrate sustainable profitability. No IPO timeline has been announced.
- **Ethical scrutiny.** As a builder of autonomous weapons systems, Anduril faces ongoing debate about lethal autonomous weapons and AI in warfare. Palmer Luckey has argued the company builds AI weapons "responsibly," but this remains a reputational risk.
- **Execution on manufacturing.** Arsenal-1 is an enormous bet. Scaling from software-centric operations to high-volume hardware manufacturing is a different discipline.
- **Palmer Luckey's political profile.** Luckey's political activity (he was pushed out of Facebook partly due to a political donation controversy in 2016) can attract attention, though it has not materially impacted business.

## Notes

- The company name "Anduril" comes from the reforged sword of Aragorn in Tolkien's _The Lord of the Rings_ -- meaning "Flame of the West." The symbolism is intentional: reforging Western defense capability.
- Anduril's HQ is in Costa Mesa, CA, with offices in Washington, DC, and expanding manufacturing in Ohio and Mississippi.
- Company headcount was reported at ~3,000+ employees as of late 2025.
- Palmer Luckey, at 33, is among the youngest founders leading a company at this valuation scale in defense.
