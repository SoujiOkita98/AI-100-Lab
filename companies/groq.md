# Groq

## Overview

Groq is an AI inference hardware company that designed the LPU (Language Processing Unit), a purpose-built chip architecture optimized for running large language models at extreme speed and low latency. Founded in 2016 by Jonathan Ross -- the creator of Google's TPU -- and a team of ex-Google engineers, Groq positioned itself as the leading alternative to Nvidia GPUs for AI inference workloads.

On December 24, 2025, Nvidia acquired most of Groq's assets and key personnel in a $20 billion licensing and acqui-hire deal -- Nvidia's largest transaction ever. The deal was structured to avoid traditional acquisition scrutiny: Groq technically remains an independent entity under new CEO Simon Edwards (former CFO), while founder Jonathan Ross, president Sunny Madra, and senior engineers joined Nvidia. As of March 2026, Nvidia has already unveiled the "Groq 3 LPU" at GTC 2026, targeting agentic AI inference for customers including OpenAI.

## Funding story

Groq's funding trajectory is a case study in how a hardware startup can reach massive scale in the AI boom -- and ultimately become too strategically valuable for the incumbent to ignore.

**2016-2018 -- Chamath's early bet.** Social Capital's Chamath Palihapitiya invested $10M in the Series A (December 2016) when Groq was valued at just $30M, and followed with $52M in the Series B (September 2018). The company was extremely secretive during this period, with virtually no public information about its chip architecture. Chamath's total ~$62M investment would eventually be worth billions in the Nvidia deal.

**2021 -- Series C ($300M at $1B+ valuation).** Tiger Global and D1 Capital led this round, making Groq a unicorn. BlackRock entered as an investor. The round came during the peak of Tiger Global's aggressive late-stage deployment, but Groq proved to be one of their better bets.

**2024 -- Series D ($640M at $2.8B valuation).** BlackRock Private Equity Partners led. The round reflected surging demand for inference compute following the ChatGPT/LLM explosion. Strategic investors Cisco, Samsung, and KDDI joined. Funds were earmarked for deploying 100,000+ LPUs into GroqCloud.

**2025 -- Peak and exit.** Three major events in rapid succession:
- *February 2025:* Saudi Arabia's HUMAIN committed $1.5 billion for LPU infrastructure deployment in Dammam, making Groq a key provider for the Kingdom's sovereign AI ambitions.
- *September 2025:* Series E raised $750M at a $6.9B valuation, led by Disruptive, with BlackRock, Neuberger Berman, Samsung, and others participating.
- *December 2025:* Nvidia's $20B acqui-hire -- roughly 2.9x the September valuation and 7x the August 2024 valuation. Structured as $17B in cash across three installments.

Total private funding raised: approximately $1.75 billion across five equity rounds plus the $1.5B Saudi infrastructure commitment.

## Technology: The LPU

The LPU (Language Processing Unit) is Groq's core innovation. Unlike Nvidia GPUs, which are general-purpose parallel processors adapted for AI, the LPU is a deterministic, single-core architecture designed specifically for sequential inference on large language models. Key properties:

- **Speed:** Groq consistently demonstrated 10-18x faster token generation than GPU-based alternatives. The GroqCloud API became famous in the developer community for near-instantaneous responses.
- **Deterministic latency:** Because the LPU avoids the non-deterministic scheduling of GPUs, inference latency is predictable -- critical for real-time applications like McLaren's F1 race analysis.
- **Simplicity:** The architecture is a Tensor Streaming Processor (TSP) -- a single large core rather than thousands of small cores. This simplifies the compiler and memory management but requires a fundamentally different software stack.
- **Limitation:** The LPU was optimized for inference, not training. This made Groq complementary to -- rather than a direct replacement for -- Nvidia's training-dominant GPU ecosystem, which is likely why Nvidia saw it as an accretive acquisition rather than a competitive threat to neutralize.

## Team

**Founding team profile:** The founding team's defining characteristic is their direct lineage from Google's TPU project. Jonathan Ross recruited 8 of the 10 original TPU engineers to join Groq, giving the startup an unmatched concentration of purpose-built AI silicon expertise.

- **Jonathan Ross (Founder & CEO, now at Nvidia):** An unconventional background -- reportedly did not finish high school through the traditional path. Studied math and CS at NYU Courant under Yann LeCun. Created the TPU as a Google 20% project. Joined Google X's Rapid Eval team to incubate Alphabet moonshots. At Groq, designed the LPU architecture. Post-acquisition, serves as Chief Software Architect at Nvidia.
- **Douglas Wightman (Co-Founder):** Former Google X engineer. Limited public profile.
- **Sunny Madra (President, now at Nvidia):** Joined as president to lead go-to-market. Previously led Ford's mobility and autonomous vehicle ventures. Followed Ross to Nvidia.
- **Simon Edwards (CFO, now CEO of remaining Groq entity):** Took over as CEO after the Nvidia deal to manage the nominally independent Groq entity.
- **Andrew Ling (Head of ML Compilers):** 10+ years at Intel and Qualcomm building compilers. PhD from University of Toronto. Leads the critical compiler stack that makes LPU programmable.

## Business model

Groq operated a dual hardware + cloud model:

1. **GroqCloud (Tokens-as-a-Service):** Consumption-based API pricing, pay-per-token. Differentiated on speed (10-18x faster than GPU alternatives) and predictable latency. Supported major open-source models (Llama, Mixtral, etc.). Over 2 million developers signed up.

2. **Infrastructure deals:** Large-scale LPU deployments for sovereign and enterprise customers. The $1.5B Saudi Arabia/HUMAIN deal is the prime example -- dedicated data centers running Groq hardware for national AI infrastructure.

3. **Hardware sales/partnerships:** LPU chips fabricated by Samsung Foundry. The original roadmap included selling LPU hardware directly, though the cloud-first model increasingly dominated.

Revenue was projected at $500M for 2025 (revised down from an ambitious $2B target). The downward revision reportedly impacted Samsung Foundry, which had been counting on Groq chip orders.

## Customers

- **HUMAIN / Saudi Arabia:** Largest customer relationship. $1.5B infrastructure commitment. Running OpenAI models on Groq infrastructure in Saudi sovereign data centers.
- **IBM:** Integrated Groq inference into Watsonx Orchestrate platform. Claims 5x faster AI workload processing vs. traditional GPUs.
- **McLaren F1:** Real-time race data analysis using Groq's low-latency inference.
- **Bell Canada:** Sovereign AI deployment across six Canadian sites serving government and enterprise customers.
- **Hugging Face:** Official inference provider partnership (June 2025), exposing Groq to millions of ML developers.
- **2M+ developers** on the GroqCloud platform as of late 2025.

## What makes them interesting

1. **The TPU-to-LPU lineage.** Groq may be the best example of a "second act" in AI hardware. The team literally built Google's TPU, then left to build something better for inference. This is not a paper architecture from a startup -- it is production silicon from the people who built the most important non-GPU AI chip in history.

2. **Speed as a product category.** Groq did not try to be cheaper or more efficient -- it tried to be *fast*. The GroqCloud API became a viral sensation in early 2024 when developers experienced near-instant LLM responses for the first time. This created a new product category: inference speed as a feature, not just a cost optimization.

3. **The Nvidia deal validates inference as a distinct market.** Nvidia paying $20B -- 2.9x the last private valuation, its largest acquisition ever -- is a strong signal that inference-optimized silicon is a strategic imperative, not a niche. Nvidia effectively admitted that GPUs alone are not optimal for the inference workload that will dominate AI compute as deployment scales.

4. **Sovereign AI infrastructure play.** The Saudi Arabia/HUMAIN relationship demonstrated a business model beyond developer APIs: becoming the inference backbone for entire nations. This "sovereign AI" angle may be the most scalable enterprise motion in AI infrastructure.

5. **Chamath's best bet.** Social Capital's ~$62M across Series A and B, at a $30M initial valuation, returned billions in the Nvidia deal -- likely one of the best venture returns of the 2020s decade.

## Notes and uncertainties

- **Revenue figures are inconsistent.** The $2B-to-$500M 2025 projection revision is a significant red flag on the pre-acquisition operating business. The $172.5M mid-2025 figure from Latka and the $500M projection from The Information may reflect different measurement periods or methodologies. Treat all pre-deal revenue figures with caution.
- **Deal structure ambiguity.** Multiple sources describe the Nvidia transaction differently -- "acquisition," "licensing deal," "acqui-hire." The structure appears designed to avoid antitrust scrutiny by maintaining the fiction that Groq remains independent. Analysts have noted this explicitly. The practical reality is that Nvidia obtained Groq's IP, key engineers, and technology; what remains of independent Groq is unclear.
- **Groq 3 LPU at GTC 2026.** As of March 2026, Nvidia has already unveiled a "Groq 3 LPU" product targeting agentic AI inference, with OpenAI as a customer. This suggests rapid integration of Groq technology into Nvidia's product line.
- **Samsung Foundry impact.** Groq's revised revenue projections reportedly dealt a blow to Samsung Foundry, which was manufacturing LPU chips. Post-Nvidia deal, it is unclear whether LPU fabrication will remain with Samsung or move to TSMC (Nvidia's primary foundry partner).
- **Not to be confused with "Grok"** -- xAI/Elon Musk's chatbot. The naming similarity has caused persistent market confusion.
