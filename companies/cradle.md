# Cradle

## Overview

Cradle is a Dutch-Swiss biotech software company that builds an AI-powered platform for protein engineering. Founded in 2021 in Amsterdam by a team combining Google/Uber product veterans with Zymergen/DSM wet-lab scientists, Cradle applies generative AI — specifically protein language models trained on billions of known protein sequences — to dramatically accelerate the process of designing new and improved proteins. The company has raised $103M across three rounds and, as of early 2026, serves six of the top 25 global pharma companies.

Cradle is essentially "GitHub Copilot for protein engineers" — it sits alongside scientists' existing workflows and suggests optimized protein sequences, reducing experimental cycles by up to 90%.

## Funding story

**2022 — Seed ($5.5M).** Index Ventures led. Notable angels included Feike Sijbesma (former DSM CEO) and Emily Leproust (Twist Bioscience founder). At this point the team was small and the product was early.

**2023 — Series A ($24M).** Again led by Index Ventures, with Kindred Capital. This round coincided with announcements of partnerships with Novo Nordisk and Ginkgo Bioworks. Angel investors included Chris Gibson (Recursion CEO) and Tom Glocer (former Thomson Reuters CEO). The caliber of angels — biotech CEOs and pharma board members — signals strong industry validation.

**2024 — Series B ($73M).** Led by IVP (Menlo Park), a significant step up in investor profile. Index and Kindred continued. This round funded wet-lab expansion in Amsterdam and US team buildout. Total raised crossed $100M.

Cradle's valuation has not been publicly disclosed at any round.

## Technology

Cradle's platform is built on generative protein language models — analogous to how GPT models work on text, but operating on amino acid sequences. The core approach:

1. **Foundation model:** Trained on billions of known protein sequences to learn the "grammar" of functional proteins — which sequences are biologically meaningful versus random noise.
2. **Generative design:** Given a target protein and desired properties (stability, binding affinity, manufacturability, expression level), the model generates candidate sequences that balance multiple optimization objectives.
3. **Active learning loop:** The platform continuously fine-tunes on customer-provided experimental results, getting smarter with each round of lab data. This creates a flywheel: more data from customers improves the model, which generates better candidates, which attracts more customers.
4. **Wet lab (Amsterdam):** Cradle operates its own lab to A/B test across protein types and generate "foundational datasets" that improve the base model for all customers.

The key value proposition is speed: traditional protein engineering requires 10-20 experimental rounds of 8-12 weeks each. Cradle claims it can cut that by up to 90%, saving hundreds of thousands of dollars in early research.

## Team

The founding team is notable for its deliberate combination of two worlds:

- **Tech/ML side:** Stef van Grieken (Google X, Google Research), Eli Bixby (Google Brain), and Jelle Prins (early Uber Europe) bring product, ML, and scaling expertise from top-tier tech companies.
- **Wet-lab/biology side:** Elise de Reus and Harmen van Rossum both came from Zymergen and DSM, bringing hands-on experience in high-throughput experimentation and industrial biotech.

This blend is intentional — the company's thesis is that protein engineering needs both strong ML and strong biology to succeed. The team has grown to approximately 107 employees as of early 2026, doubling in the past year. Recent key hires include Sam Partovi as CCO (life sciences veteran) to lead commercial expansion, particularly in the US.

Four of five founders are Dutch, and the company maintains its roots in Amsterdam while expanding a US presence.

## Business model

Cradle runs a **pure SaaS model** — customers pay a software subscription fee. This is a deliberate and strategically important choice. In biotech, partnerships often involve royalties, revenue sharing, milestone payments, or IP co-ownership, which creates friction and limits adoption. Cradle avoids all of this: customers keep 100% of their IP.

This makes Cradle an easy "yes" for pharma procurement teams, since there are no downstream financial entanglements. It also means Cradle can scale across many customers and industries without the complexity of managing deal-by-deal economics.

The Ginkgo Bioworks partnership extends this further — users without their own labs can access Cradle's AI plus Ginkgo's lab infrastructure, lowering the barrier to entry.

## What makes them interesting

1. **SaaS model in a royalty-driven industry.** Most AI-for-biology companies entangle themselves in IP and revenue-share deals. Cradle's clean SaaS model is a structural advantage for adoption speed and customer trust. It is also more predictable and scalable as a business.

2. **Customer quality is exceptional for their stage.** Six of the top 25 pharma companies, including J&J, Novo Nordisk, and AbbVie, are paying customers. This is remarkable for a company that is only four years old with $103M raised. These are not pilot programs — 50+ R&D programs are running on the platform.

3. **Active learning flywheel.** Each customer's experimental data improves Cradle's models, which improves results for all customers. This is a genuine network effect that is rare in biotech software.

4. **Founding team composition.** The deliberate mix of Google/Uber tech talent with Zymergen/DSM wet-lab scientists is uncommon. Many AI-for-biology startups are founded by either pure ML researchers or pure biologists — the combination gives Cradle credibility on both sides.

5. **Multi-industry applicability.** Proteins are not just a pharma problem. Cradle's customers span pharmaceuticals, chemicals, food, agriculture, and materials. This gives the company a much larger TAM than pure drug-discovery AI startups.

6. **Amsterdam as a biotech hub.** Operating from the Netherlands (with Zurich and US presence) gives Cradle access to Europe's strong biotech talent pool and proximity to major European pharma companies (Novo Nordisk, Novonesis, Grifols), while IVP-led Series B signals serious US expansion.

## Competitive landscape

Cradle competes with other AI protein design companies including Absci, Generate Biomedicines (acquired by Novartis), EvolutionaryScale (ESM models), and in-house ML teams at large pharma companies. Its differentiation is the SaaS model (vs. drug development or co-development approaches), the active learning loop with customer data, and the breadth of application beyond pharma.
