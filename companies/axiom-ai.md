# Axiom

## Overview

Axiom is a "Verified AI" startup that uses formal mathematics to prove whether AI-generated code and other outputs are correct. Founded by Carina Hong, a 25-year-old Stanford PhD student and MIT graduate, the company has raised $264 million total across seed and Series A rounds, reaching a $1.6 billion valuation in March 2026.

The core insight is simple but powerful: as AI-generated code proliferates (via Cursor, GitHub Copilot, Claude Code, etc.), the ability to formally verify that code is correct becomes increasingly critical. Axiom applies techniques from formal mathematics -- mathematical proof systems -- to verify AI outputs, addressing one of the most fundamental challenges in AI deployment: reliability.

## Funding story

**October 2025 -- $64M Seed at $300M valuation.** Led by B Capital, with Greycroft, Madrona, and Menlo Ventures participating. Unusually large seed round reflecting the founder's pedigree and the strength of the thesis.

**March 2026 -- $200M Series A at $1.6B valuation.** Led by Menlo Ventures, with all existing investors following on plus Toyota Ventures and Triatomic Capital. The 5x valuation jump in under 6 months signals strong technical progress or market demand signals.

## Team

- **Carina Hong (CEO & Founder):** 25 years old. Stanford math PhD student. MIT graduate. Her age and academic profile are reminiscent of other wunderkind founders (Alexandr Wang of Scale AI, founded at 19). The combination of deep mathematical talent and entrepreneurial ambition is rare.

## What makes them interesting

1. **Verification is the meta-problem.** As AI-generated code scales across every industry, the question "is this code correct?" becomes the single most important quality gate. Axiom is positioned at this chokepoint. Every AI coding assistant creates demand for Axiom's verification.

2. **Formal verification meets AI is technically non-obvious.** Most AI companies are building bigger models. Axiom is using a different branch of computer science -- formal methods, proof theory -- to solve a problem that bigger models alone cannot solve (you cannot use AI to verify AI without a formal framework).

3. **The founder story is compelling.** A 25-year-old Stanford math PhD student raising $264M and building a unicorn is the kind of narrative that attracts talent, press, and enterprise interest.

4. **TAM grows with AI adoption.** Every line of AI-generated code that enters production is a potential Axiom customer. As Gartner and others predict that 80%+ of code will be AI-generated by 2028, Axiom's addressable market expands proportionally.

5. **Toyota Ventures participation** in the Series A is interesting -- suggests automotive/safety-critical use cases (autonomous vehicles, manufacturing) where formal verification is not optional.

## Notes

- **Technical moat question:** Formal verification of arbitrary code is an extremely hard problem (provably undecidable in the general case). Axiom's ability to deliver practical, scalable verification for real-world AI-generated code -- not just toy examples -- is the key technical question.
- **Single founder risk.** Carina Hong is the sole named founder. For a company at this scale and ambition, the depth of the leadership bench will matter.
- **Competitive landscape:** Microsoft Research, Google DeepMind, and academic labs all work on formal verification. Axiom's advantage is focus and speed as a startup, but the big labs could commoditize this space.
- **Confidence is medium** due to limited public information about product maturity, customer adoption, and technical benchmarks.
