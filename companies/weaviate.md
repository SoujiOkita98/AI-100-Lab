# Weaviate

## Overview

Weaviate is an open-source, AI-native vector database headquartered in Amsterdam, Netherlands. The company builds infrastructure for storing and searching data using vector embeddings -- the numerical representations that machine learning models use to understand meaning. Founded in 2019 as SeMI Technologies by Bob van Luijt, Etienne Dilocker, and Micha Verhagen, the company was spun out of ING Labs and later rebranded to match its flagship product. Weaviate is written in Go, is cloud-native, and differentiates through its modular architecture, native multi-tenancy, and combined vector + keyword search capabilities.

As of March 2026, Weaviate has raised approximately $117.6M in total funding across four rounds and was last valued at $200M (Series C, October 2025). The open-source project has achieved 14,000+ GitHub stars and 5+ million downloads. The company operates as a remote-first organization with ~81 employees globally, down from a peak of ~104 in 2024.

## Funding story

**2020 -- Seed ($1.6M).** Led by Zetta Venture Partners with ING Ventures participating. Raised under the original company name SeMI Technologies. This was early-stage capital to build the open-source project into a commercial product.

**2022 -- Series A ($16M).** Co-led by New Enterprise Associates (NEA) and Cortical Ventures, with Zetta and ING Ventures also participating. Positioned as part of "a new wave of AI-first database tech." The company was still operating as SeMI Technologies.

**2023 -- Series B ($50M).** Led by Index Ventures, with Battery Ventures joining and all existing investors participating. This round came during the generative AI explosion, as enterprises rushed to build RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) pipelines. By this point, Weaviate reported 2,000+ companies in production and 2M+ open-source downloads. The company had rebranded from SeMI Technologies to Weaviate.

**2025 -- Series C ($50M at $200M valuation).** Led by Battery Ventures and Zetta Venture Partners, announced October 2025. The $200M valuation is notably modest for a company at this stage in the AI infrastructure space, suggesting either disciplined fundraising or investor caution about the competitive vector database market.

**Note on total raised:** Some sources (e.g., Clay, Crunchbase) report total funding as $67.6M rather than $117.6M. This discrepancy likely stems from how earlier rounds under the SeMI Technologies entity are counted. The $117.6M figure sums all four publicly reported rounds.

## Team

**Bob van Luijt (Co-Founder & CEO):** One of the most unusual founder backgrounds in enterprise infrastructure. Born in Bergen op Zoom, Netherlands, Van Luijt started coding and running a software company at age 15. He then pursued a formal education in jazz and electronic composition at the Artez Institute of the Arts, followed by Berklee College of Music in the US (on scholarship). He later completed the Harvard Business School Program of Management Excellence. He ran a consultancy called Kubrickology -- named after filmmaker Stanley Kubrick -- before starting the Weaviate open-source project in March 2016. He shut down the consultancy to co-found SeMI Technologies in 2019, which became Weaviate. Van Luijt is also an angel investor.

**Etienne Dilocker (Co-Founder & CTO):** German, based in Mannheim. Deep-tech background in distributed systems, Golang, database engineering, and cloud-native architecture. Former freelance software engineer and managing director of his own consulting firm. His current research focuses on auto-scaling databases and resource-efficient vector indexing -- core problems for making vector search economically viable at enterprise scale.

**Micha Verhagen (Co-Founder):** Dutch. Limited public information available about his current role and background.

The team has roots in ING Labs, the innovation arm of the Dutch banking giant ING, which provided both the initial environment and seed-stage investment. The company is remote-first, with team members distributed globally despite the Amsterdam headquarters (Prinsengracht 769A).

## Business model

Weaviate follows the standard open-core model:

- **Open source (free):** The core vector database is BSD-3 licensed. Users can self-host on their own infrastructure with no licensing cost. This drives adoption and community growth.
- **Weaviate Cloud -- Shared Cloud:** Multi-tenant, pay-as-you-go managed service. Low barrier to entry for startups and small teams.
- **Weaviate Cloud -- Dedicated Cloud:** Fully managed dedicated resources for enterprise production workloads. Includes RBAC, SOC 2 compliance, HIPAA compliance, and enterprise SLAs.
- **Bring Your Own Cloud (BYOC):** Weaviate manages the database on the customer's existing cloud infrastructure, addressing data sovereignty and compliance requirements.

Enterprise pricing is structured around annual contracts denominated in AI Units (AIUs), with AWS and Azure Marketplace contracts starting at $10,000/year. A major pricing overhaul took effect October 27, 2025, expanding billing dimensions to include storage, backups, and regional pricing variations.

## Customers

Weaviate's most detailed public case study is **Instabase**, which runs 50,000+ tenants on Weaviate and indexes 450+ data types for individual customer solutions. Other confirmed customers include **Red Hat**, **NetApp**, **Morningstar**, **Cisco**, **Bunq** (Dutch neobank), and **Stack AI**.

Use cases span enterprise search, RAG pipelines, recommendation systems, multi-modal search, and generative AI applications. Weaviate's native multi-tenancy architecture makes it particularly suited for SaaS platforms that need to isolate data across many customers (as demonstrated by the Instabase case).

## What makes them interesting

1. **Genuinely unusual founder story.** Bob van Luijt is a jazz-trained musician who started coding at 15, studied at Berklee, and then built an enterprise database company. This is not a typical infrastructure founder profile -- it brings a different perspective on design, user experience, and systemic thinking. The Kubrickology consultancy name hints at an obsession with precision and craft.

2. **Early mover with staying power.** The Weaviate open-source project started in 2016 -- years before "vector database" became a buzzword. By the time the generative AI wave hit in 2022-2023, Weaviate had a mature, battle-tested codebase. Many competitors (Chroma, Qdrant, LanceDB) started much later.

3. **Architecture designed for multi-tenancy at scale.** Weaviate's native multi-tenancy is a genuine differentiator. Rather than bolting multi-tenancy on top, it is built into the core architecture, enabling efficient isolation of potentially millions of tenants. This matters enormously for SaaS companies building AI features.

4. **Go-based performance with modular ML integration.** Written in Go for speed and concurrency, Weaviate combines vector search (HNSW, flat, dynamic indexes), keyword search (BM25), and hybrid search in one system. It supports pluggable vectorization modules (OpenAI, Cohere, Hugging Face, etc.) so users can swap models without changing their data layer.

5. **European DNA in a US-dominated market.** Headquartered in Amsterdam with ING Labs origins, Weaviate brings European sensibilities around data privacy and sovereignty. The BYOC offering and compliance posture (SOC 2, HIPAA) are natural extensions of this DNA and resonate with regulated industries.

6. **Disciplined valuation.** At $200M (Series C, October 2025), Weaviate's valuation is notably restrained compared to AI infrastructure peers. For context, Pinecone was valued at $750M in April 2023 on similar revenue. This could indicate either investor caution about the vector database market's commoditization risk or a deliberate choice to avoid overvaluation -- both of which may serve the company well long-term.

## Risks and challenges

- **Fierce competition from all directions.** Pinecone (managed), Milvus/Zilliz (open-source + cloud), Qdrant, Chroma, and pgvector (PostgreSQL extension) all compete directly. Every major cloud provider and database vendor (MongoDB Atlas Vector Search, Elasticsearch, Redis) now offers vector search as a feature. The standalone vector database market may contract.

- **Employee count declining.** The drop from ~104 to ~81 employees between 2024 and early 2026 warrants attention. In a competitive market where talent is the primary asset, headcount shrinkage could signal challenges with retention, revenue growth, or strategic direction.

- **Revenue remains modest.** $12.3M in 2024 revenue against $117.6M raised means the company has not yet demonstrated the kind of revenue acceleration that justifies continued independent operation at scale. The open-core model often struggles with conversion rates.

- **Open-source double-edged sword.** The BSD-3 license is extremely permissive -- any cloud provider could offer a managed Weaviate service without paying Weaviate. This is the same risk that led companies like MongoDB, Elastic, and Redis to adopt more restrictive licenses. Weaviate has not made such a move.
