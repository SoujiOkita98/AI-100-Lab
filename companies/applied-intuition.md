# Applied Intuition

## Overview

Applied Intuition builds AI-powered software for developing, validating, and deploying autonomous systems. Founded in 2017 by Qasar Younis and Peter Ludwig, the company started with a focused thesis: the autonomous vehicle industry's biggest bottleneck was not hardware or algorithms but the **simulation and testing infrastructure** needed to validate them safely at scale. That original insight -- sell the picks and shovels, not mine the gold -- has proven remarkably durable.

From its base in Mountain View, CA, Applied Intuition has expanded from AV simulation tooling into a full-stack autonomy platform spanning automotive ADAS, defense, trucking, construction, mining, and agriculture. As of early 2026, it serves 18 of the top 20 global automakers and holds contracts with the U.S. Department of Defense.

## Team

**Qasar Younis** (CEO) brings an unusual background for a Silicon Valley founder. Born on a farm in Pakistan, he immigrated to the US at age five and grew up in the Detroit area -- the heart of American automaking. He studied engineering at the General Motors Institute (now Kettering University), where he managed a V6 engine line on the factory floor. After an MBA at Harvard Business School, he worked as an engineer at GM and then Bosch in Japan, giving him firsthand experience with the glacial pace of automotive software development. He co-founded Talkbin (acquired by Google in 2011), then served as COO of Y Combinator under Sam Altman, where he gained a panoramic view of the startup ecosystem before deciding to build again.

**Peter Ludwig** (CTO) is a University of Michigan CS grad who spent five years at Google, working on Google Maps and Android Automotive and building sensors for Street View cars. He and Younis are both Michiganders who grew up in the orbit of the auto industry.

The founding story is a convergence of two threads: **deep automotive domain knowledge** (Younis at GM/Bosch, Ludwig at Google Automotive) and **Silicon Valley product sensibility** (Younis at YC, Ludwig at Google). They saw that automakers were spending billions on autonomous driving but lacked the simulation and tooling infrastructure to test safely at scale -- a problem that software could solve.

The company's blog describes "31 founders at Applied Intuition," reflecting a culture that emphasizes an ownership mentality across leadership. The team has grown to approximately 1,400 employees across 12 offices on 5 continents, with major engineering hubs in Mountain View, Ann Arbor, and international offices in Stuttgart, Munich, London, Stockholm, Bangalore, Seoul, and Tokyo.

## Products

1. **Simulation & Validation Platform** -- The original product. High-fidelity simulation for ADAS and autonomous driving: sensor modeling, scenario generation, large-scale regression testing. This is the core wedge that got Applied Intuition into 18 of the top 20 automakers.

2. **SDS for Automotive** -- Launched in 2025. A full end-to-end ADAS/autonomy stack for passenger vehicles combining a unified neural architecture, white-box transparency, and a data engine. This represents a strategic expansion from "tooling vendor" to "stack provider."

3. **Axion** (Defense) -- Launched May 2025. An all-domain data engine and simulation toolkit for defense. Platform-agnostic tooling for developing and validating autonomous systems across land, air, sea, and space.

4. **Acuity** (Defense) -- Launched May 2025. Onboard autonomy software for unmanned military systems, built on the acquired EpiSci technology. Designed to plug into platforms ranging from ground vehicles to fighter jets.

## What makes them interesting

1. **Picks-and-shovels positioning in the autonomy gold rush.** While many AV companies burned cash trying to build robotaxis, Applied Intuition sold the simulation and testing tools that *every* autonomy developer needs. This is a structurally advantageous position: they win regardless of which OEM or AV stack prevails.

2. **Revenue growth is extraordinary.** ~$207M ARR in 2023, ~$415M in 2024, reportedly ~$800M in 2025 -- roughly doubling every year. At 85% gross margins on a SaaS model, this is elite unit economics. If the trajectory holds, they could cross $1B ARR in 2026.

3. **Platform expansion from tooling to stack.** The launch of SDS for Automotive signals a shift from selling simulation tools to selling the autonomy stack itself. This dramatically expands the addressable market but also puts Applied Intuition in more direct competition with its own customers' internal efforts.

4. **Defense as a second growth vector.** The February 2025 acquisition of EpiSci and the May 2025 launch of Axion/Acuity products position Applied Intuition as a serious defense autonomy player. The all-domain (land/air/sea/space) ambition mirrors Anduril's playbook, and the December 2025 SNC partnership suggests real traction. Defense diversifies revenue away from the cyclical automotive industry.

5. **Detroit roots, Silicon Valley execution.** The founders' Michigan/automotive backgrounds give them genuine credibility with OEM customers who are often skeptical of tech companies that don't understand the industry's constraints (safety certification, supply chains, regulatory complexity). This cultural bridge is hard to replicate.

6. **Capital efficiency.** Applied Intuition reached a $15B valuation on $1.2B raised -- a 12.5x ratio that reflects strong organic growth rather than capital-intensive operations. Compare this to AV companies like Cruise or Argo AI, which burned billions before shutting down or restructuring.

## Risks and open questions

- **Customer concentration.** Selling to "18 of the top 20 automakers" sounds impressive, but revenue concentration among a small number of large accounts is a typical risk for enterprise software companies at this stage. Contract sizes and renewal rates are not publicly disclosed.
- **Stack vs. tooling tension.** As Applied Intuition moves from selling simulation tools to selling a full autonomy stack (SDS), it risks competing with customers who are building their own stacks. Managing this transition without alienating the tooling customer base is a key strategic challenge.
- **Defense execution.** Defense is a different sales motion (long procurement cycles, security clearances, government relationships). The EpiSci acquisition brings domain expertise, but scaling a defense business alongside a commercial automotive business requires different organizational muscles.
- **IPO timing.** At $15B valuation and strong growth, an IPO seems likely in the medium term, but no timeline has been announced. Late-stage private investors (BlackRock, Fidelity, sovereign wealth) suggest the company may stay private longer.
- **Valuation relative to revenue.** At ~$800M 2025 revenue and $15B valuation, the company trades at roughly 19x revenue -- rich but defensible if growth continues at current rates. A growth slowdown would pressure this multiple.

## Notes
