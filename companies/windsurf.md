# Windsurf (formerly Codeium / Exafunction)

## Overview

Windsurf was an AI-powered code editor and developer tools company that grew from zero to over 1 million developers in four months after launching its IDE in November 2024. The company became the center of one of the most dramatic acquisition sagas in recent AI history, with OpenAI, Google, and Cognition all vying for pieces of the company in mid-2025. It is a textbook case of a successful pivot, strong product-led growth, and the intense talent wars defining the current AI era.

## Founding and Origins

Varun Mohan and Douglas Chen met in middle school and later both attended MIT. Mohan earned a Master of Engineering in Computer Science and a BS in EECS (with a math minor) from MIT. He went on to work as a Tech Lead Manager at Nuro (2018-2021), managing large-scale deep learning infrastructure for autonomous vehicles. Chen worked at Meta, building software tools for VR headsets including the Oculus Quest.

In June 2021, Mohan and Chen founded **Exafunction**, a startup focused on optimizing GPU utilization at scale -- essentially GPU virtualization infrastructure for AI workloads. The company raised a $25M Series A led by Greenoaks in April 2022.

## The Pivot: Exafunction to Codeium

As generative AI models surged in 2022, Mohan and Chen recognized that the bigger opportunity was not in GPU infrastructure but in building end-user applications on top of that infrastructure. In 2022, they pivoted the company to **Codeium**, an AI-powered code completion and developer productivity tool. The first beta launched in October 2022 as an extension for existing IDEs (VS Code, JetBrains, etc.).

This was a bold pivot -- the team abandoned a funded infrastructure product to compete in a market where GitHub Copilot (backed by Microsoft/OpenAI) already had significant traction. Their bet was that a model-agnostic, free-tier-first approach could win developer hearts.

## The Windsurf IDE and Rebrand

In November 2024, Codeium launched the **Windsurf Editor**, its own full IDE (a fork of VS Code), designed to deeply integrate AI capabilities beyond what extensions could offer -- including "agentic" flows where the AI could autonomously navigate codebases, make multi-file edits, and reason about context. The company subsequently rebranded entirely to **Windsurf** in early 2025.

The Windsurf IDE reached over 1 million developers within four months of launch, a remarkable adoption curve that attracted significant industry attention.

## Business Model

Windsurf operated a **freemium SaaS / product-led growth** model:

- **Free tier** ($0): Generous free usage to drive individual developer adoption
- **Pro** ($15/month): Enhanced capabilities for individual power users
- **Teams** ($30/user/month): Collaboration features
- **Enterprise**: Self-hosted deployments, enhanced security, custom integrations, negotiated pricing

The go-to-market was classically bottom-up: individual developers adopted the free tier, usage spread within organizations, and enterprise sales followed. Enterprise customers included Zillow, Dell, and Anduril among 350+ accounts. By July 2025, Windsurf had reached **$82M ARR**, up from ~$40M in February 2025, with enterprise ARR doubling quarter-over-quarter.

## The Acquisition Drama (Mid-2025)

This is where Windsurf's story becomes extraordinary.

### OpenAI's $3B Bid

In May 2025, OpenAI entered into an exclusivity agreement to acquire Windsurf for approximately **$3 billion** -- which would have been OpenAI's largest acquisition ever. The deal made strategic sense: OpenAI wanted a full-featured developer IDE to compete with Microsoft's GitHub Copilot, giving it a direct-to-developer product.

However, the deal collapsed due to a fundamental conflict with Microsoft. Under OpenAI's partnership agreement, Microsoft holds extensive IP rights to OpenAI's technology, including acquired IP. Microsoft expected those rights to extend to Windsurf's technology, but OpenAI refused -- viewing Windsurf's innovations as a competitive asset precisely *against* Microsoft's GitHub Copilot. The exclusivity period expired in early July 2025 without a deal.

### Google's $2.4B Acqui-Hire

On July 11, 2025, Google swooped in with a different kind of deal. Rather than acquiring the company, Google agreed to pay approximately **$2.4 billion** to:
- Hire CEO Varun Mohan, co-founder Douglas Chen, and roughly 40 top engineers into **Google DeepMind**
- Obtain a **non-exclusive license** to certain Windsurf technology

Critically, Google did not acquire Windsurf as a company and took no equity stake. The license was non-exclusive, meaning Windsurf's technology could still be licensed to others.

### Cognition's Acquisition of the Remaining Business

Just three days later, on July 14, 2025, **Cognition** (the startup behind the Devin autonomous coding agent) signed a definitive agreement to acquire the remainder of Windsurf -- its product, brand, IP, trademark, and remaining team. The acquisition price was not publicly disclosed. Cognition committed to:
- 100% of Windsurf employees participating financially in the deal
- Waiving all vesting cliffs and fully accelerating vesting for work to date
- Continuing to operate Windsurf's product in the near term while integrating capabilities into Cognition's offerings

By September 2025, Cognition was valued at $10.2 billion, just two months after the Windsurf purchase.

## What Makes Windsurf Interesting (Stanford GSB Research Lens)

1. **Masterclass in pivoting**: The Exafunction-to-Codeium pivot is a case study in recognizing when the market has shifted. The founders abandoned a funded product to pursue a bigger opportunity in a crowded market -- and won.

2. **Competing against incumbents with free**: Windsurf's free tier was not a loss leader; it was a strategic weapon against GitHub Copilot. By removing the cost barrier, they achieved viral developer adoption that enterprise sales could not have replicated.

3. **Speed of execution**: Launching an IDE in November 2024 and hitting 1M users in four months is exceptional. The team's deep infrastructure background (GPU optimization, autonomous vehicle systems) gave them technical advantages in model serving and latency optimization.

4. **The three-way split as a new M&A archetype**: The Windsurf outcome -- where founders and key talent went to Google, the business went to Cognition, and OpenAI walked away empty-handed -- may represent a new template for how AI startup acquisitions unfold when talent is the primary asset.

5. **OpenAI-Microsoft tension made visible**: The Windsurf saga publicly exposed the structural tension in the OpenAI-Microsoft relationship around IP rights, particularly in the developer tools space where their interests directly conflict.

6. **Valuation trajectory**: From $500M (Jan 2024) to $1.25B (Aug 2024) to a $3B acquisition offer (May 2025) -- roughly 6x in 18 months -- illustrates the velocity of value creation in AI developer tools.

## Key Uncertainties

- The exact terms and price of Cognition's acquisition of the remaining Windsurf business were not publicly disclosed.
- The precise size of the seed round and its investors are not well-documented in public sources.
- It is unclear how much of the $2.4B Google paid went to founders vs. investors vs. the company. Some reporting from TechCrunch (August 2025) began to detail the split, but full terms remain private.
- The long-term fate of the Windsurf product under Cognition ownership is still evolving as of March 2026.
