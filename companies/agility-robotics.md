# Agility Robotics

## One-liner

Agility Robotics (now rebranded to "Agility") builds Digit, a bipedal humanoid robot designed for warehouse logistics and material handling, and is the first company to deploy humanoid robots in live commercial operations at scale.

## Company Overview

Agility Robotics was founded in 2015 as a spinout from Oregon State University's Dynamic Robotics Laboratory. The company is rooted in over a decade of academic research by co-founder Jonathan Hurst on the physics of legged locomotion, beginning with his Ph.D. work at Carnegie Mellon University's Robotics Institute. The three co-founders -- Hurst, Damion Shelton, and Mikhail Jones -- first developed Cassie, a legless-torso biped that proved dynamic bipedal walking was commercially viable, before evolving the platform into Digit, a full humanoid with arms, torso, and perception systems.

The company is headquartered in Salem, Oregon, where it operates **RoboFab**, opened in late 2023 as the world's first dedicated humanoid robot factory. The 70,000 sq ft facility is designed to scale to 10,000+ units per year and could employ 500+ workers at full capacity. RoboFab is a capital-light assembly facility -- no heavy industrial equipment -- and notably, Digit robots work on their own assembly line.

In March 2024, the company brought on **Peggy Johnson** (former Microsoft EVP, former Magic Leap CEO) as CEO, with co-founder Shelton transitioning to Chairman. This signaled a shift from R&D-mode to commercial scaling. In March 2026, the company rebranded from "Agility Robotics" to simply "Agility," reflecting ambitions beyond hardware into software and fleet orchestration services.

As of early 2026, the company has approximately 332 employees and annual revenue of roughly $14.7M (per Growjo estimate -- treat with caution, as Agility does not publicly disclose revenue).

## Technology

### Digit Humanoid Robot

- **Height:** 5'9" (175 cm)
- **Payload:** 35 lbs (current generation); next-gen targeting 50 lbs
- **Locomotion:** Two spring-loaded legs enabling bipedal walking over ramps, curbs, dock plates, and uneven surfaces that stop wheeled robots
- **Manipulation:** Two arms for picking, placing, loading/unloading totes and containers
- **Perception:** Cameras, LiDAR, and IMU providing 360-degree environmental awareness
- **Battery life:** 2-3 hours heavy use; up to 8 hours for lighter tasks; returns to autonomous charging docks
- **Price:** ~$250K per unit (direct purchase); also available via Robotics-as-a-Service (RaaS) model
- **Units deployed:** ~100 as of 2025 (unconfirmed exact figure)

### Agility Arc Platform

Arc is the company's cloud-based fleet orchestration platform that manages mixed automation systems including Digit humanoids, autonomous mobile robots (AMRs), conveyors, and collaborative robots. Arc represents a software-driven revenue expansion opportunity beyond hardware sales.

### Evolution Path

1. **Cassie (2017):** Legless-torso biped; proof of concept for dynamic bipedal locomotion
2. **Digit v1-v2 (2019-2020):** Added torso, arms, and basic manipulation
3. **Digit (commercial, 2023-present):** Full humanoid with perception, manipulation, and autonomous navigation for warehouse environments
4. **Next-gen Digit (expected late 2025 / early 2026):** 50 lb payload, improved battery life, enhanced manipulation

## Business Model

Agility operates a **hybrid B2B model**:

1. **Direct hardware sales:** ~$250K per unit including integration support. Targets customers ready for immediate ownership.
2. **Robotics-as-a-Service (RaaS):** Monthly subscription covering robots, maintenance, software updates, and support. Reduces customer CapEx barriers and creates recurring revenue. Toyota Canada's deployment uses this model.
3. **Software/platform revenue:** Arc cloud platform for fleet orchestration, creating stickiness and upsell potential.
4. **Leasing agreements and service contracts:** Additional revenue streams for ongoing maintenance and support.

The economic value proposition is stark: after 18 months of testing at Amazon's Sumner, WA facility, Digit reportedly achieves a **98% task success rate** at an operating cost of **$10-12/hour** versus ~$30/hour for equivalent human labor (source: DCVC). This 3x cost advantage, if sustained at scale, underpins the commercial case.

## Founding Story & Academic Origins

The company's intellectual roots begin at **Carnegie Mellon University**, where Jonathan Hurst and Damion Shelton were Ph.D. students in robotics. After graduating, Hurst joined **Oregon State University** as a professor, co-founded the OSU Robotics Institute, and established the Dynamic Robotics Laboratory. His research focused on the physics of bipedal locomotion -- understanding how to make robots walk dynamically rather than quasi-statically (the approach used by earlier humanoids like Honda's ASIMO).

The lab produced **Cassie** (later **ATRIAS**), a bird-like biped that demonstrated efficient, dynamic walking. In 2015, Hurst, Shelton, and Hurst's graduate student Mikhail Jones spun the technology out of OSU to form Agility Robotics. The company brought Cassie's IP with it and evolved the platform into the commercially-oriented Digit.

This academic pedigree -- over a decade of NSF/DARPA-funded fundamental research before commercialization -- distinguishes Agility from venture-funded startups like Figure AI that started with commercial ambitions from day one. Agility's approach was science-first, company-second.

## What Makes Agility Interesting

1. **First-mover in real commercial deployment.** While competitors demo in labs, Agility has Digit operating in live Amazon, GXO, and Toyota production environments. Over 100,000 tasks completed at Amazon alone. This is the strongest claim to real-world humanoid deployment at commercial scale as of early 2026.

2. **Purpose-built for logistics, not general-purpose hype.** Rather than chasing the "general-purpose humanoid" narrative, Agility deliberately narrowed to warehouse/logistics material handling -- an enormous TAM (~$200B+ in warehouse labor annually in the US alone) with clear, measurable ROI. The $10-12/hr operating cost vs. $30/hr human labor is a compelling economic wedge.

3. **RoboFab manufacturing advantage.** The world's first humanoid robot factory, operational since late 2023, gives Agility a manufacturing learning curve head start over competitors still in prototype stages.

4. **Academic depth + commercial execution.** 10+ years of fundamental locomotion research from OSU/CMU, combined with a pragmatic commercial team (Peggy Johnson from Microsoft/Magic Leap). This blend of deep science and enterprise go-to-market is unusual.

5. **Amazon relationship as strategic moat.** Amazon is both investor (via Industrial Innovation Fund) and anchor customer. Given Amazon's 1,000+ fulfillment centers globally, even modest Digit penetration represents massive scale potential.

6. **Legs > wheels in unstructured environments.** Digit's bipedal locomotion lets it navigate ramps, curbs, stairs, and dock plates that stop wheeled AMRs. This gives it access to brownfield facilities not designed for automation -- the vast majority of existing warehouses.

## Key Risks & Open Questions

- **Revenue vs. valuation gap.** ~$14.7M estimated revenue against a ~$2.1B valuation implies the market is pricing in dramatic growth that has not yet materialized. [Revenue figure is an estimate; treat with uncertainty.]
- **Battery life limitations.** 2-3 hours of heavy use is a constraint for 10+ hour warehouse shifts. Autonomous recharging mitigates but does not eliminate this issue.
- **Competition intensifying.** Figure AI ($39B valuation), Tesla Optimus (manufacturing scale), Boston Dynamics (Atlas), 1X Technologies, and Chinese entrants like Unitree are all accelerating. The window for first-mover advantage is narrowing.
- **CEO transition risk.** Moving from founder-CEO (Shelton) to professional CEO (Johnson) is a common inflection point. The rapid title changes for Shelton (CEO -> President -> Chief Engagement Officer -> Chairman) bear watching.
- **Scaling from 100 to 10,000 units.** RoboFab has capacity but demand must follow. The jump from ~100 deployed units to thousands is a significant commercial and operational challenge.
- **Melonee Wise departure.** Losing the CTO/CPO (a warehouse robotics veteran who founded Fetch Robotics) in early 2025 is notable. Current CTO role is unclear from public sources.

## Competitive Landscape Position

Among humanoid robotics companies, Agility occupies a distinctive niche: the **practical, logistics-focused, already-deployed** player. Compared to peers:

- **Figure AI:** Higher valuation ($39B vs $2.1B), more capital, broader ambitions (manufacturing + consumer), but less real-world deployment history.
- **Tesla Optimus:** Unmatched manufacturing scale potential, but humanoid program is younger and less commercially deployed.
- **Boston Dynamics (Atlas):** Decades of R&D excellence, but commercial humanoid deployment has been limited; strong in quadrupeds (Spot).
- **1X Technologies:** Norwegian competitor with NEO humanoid; backed by OpenAI; focused on general-purpose applications.

Agility's moat is execution: it has the most hours of real commercial humanoid operation of any company in the world.
