# World Labs

## Overview

World Labs is building Large World Models (LWMs) -- AI systems that can perceive, generate, and reason about 3D environments with what the company calls "spatial intelligence." Founded in January 2024 by Fei-Fei Li and three leading computer vision/graphics researchers (Justin Johnson, Ben Mildenhall, Christoph Lassner), the company emerged from stealth in September 2024 with $230 million in funding and a $1 billion valuation. By February 2026, it had raised an additional $1 billion at an approximate $5 billion valuation.

The core thesis: while large language models (LLMs) predict the next token in a text sequence, world models predict what a 3D environment should look like and how it will behave over time. This is a fundamentally different modality -- moving from 2D language/image generation into persistent, navigable, physically coherent 3D spaces. If LLMs gave machines the ability to "read," World Labs aims to give machines the ability to "see" and "understand" the physical world.

## Funding story

**September 2024 -- Out of stealth ($230M at $1B valuation).** World Labs emerged publicly with $230 million across a seed round (~$100M) and a Series A extension (~$130M), led by Andreessen Horowitz, NEA, and Radical Ventures. The investor list read like an AI hall of fame: NVentures (Nvidia), AMD Ventures, Adobe Ventures, Databricks Ventures, Salesforce Ventures, plus angels including Marc Benioff, Jeff Dean, Geoffrey Hinton, Reid Hoffman, Eric Schmidt, and Ashton Kutcher. Cisco also invested separately in 2025.

**February 2026 -- Series B ($1B at ~$5B valuation).** A blockbuster round that included a $200M strategic investment from Autodesk, with Andreessen Horowitz, Nvidia, AMD, Fidelity, Emerson Collective, and Sea Group also participating. The ~$5B valuation (per Bloomberg; not officially confirmed) represented a 5x increase in roughly 16 months. The Autodesk partnership is particularly significant -- it signals that a major incumbent in 3D design tools sees World Labs' technology as complementary rather than competitive, at least initially.

**Total raised: ~$1.23 billion.** For a company that only shipped its first product in November 2025, this is an aggressive capital position, reflecting both Fei-Fei Li's fundraising power and investor conviction in the spatial intelligence thesis.

## Team

The founding team is unusual in AI startups: all four co-founders are deeply technical researchers with specific, complementary expertise in 3D vision and graphics -- not generalist ML engineers or business operators.

- **Fei-Fei Li (CEO):** The "Godmother of AI." Born in Beijing, raised in Chengdu, immigrated to the U.S. at 16, worked in her family's dry-cleaning shop in New Jersey, earned a full scholarship to Princeton, PhD from Caltech. Created ImageNet, directed Stanford AI Lab, co-founded Stanford HAI. Her memoir "The Worlds I See" (2023) made the case for spatial intelligence as AI's next frontier. She remains on the Stanford faculty -- a dual role that gives World Labs academic credibility but could raise questions about full-time operational focus.

- **Justin Johnson:** Fei-Fei Li's PhD student at Stanford. Left an assistant professorship at University of Michigan and a research scientist role at Meta to co-found. Known for style transfer and visual reasoning research.

- **Ben Mildenhall:** Co-creator of NeRF (Neural Radiance Fields), one of the most influential papers in recent computer vision history. PhD from UC Berkeley (Hertz Fellow). Previously at Google Research. NeRF is directly foundational to World Labs' technical approach.

- **Christoph Lassner:** Creator of Pulsar, the sphere-based differentiable renderer that underpins PyTorch3D and contributed to the development of Gaussian Splatting. Previously led research at Meta Reality Labs and Epic Games. German background.

The intellectual throughline: Johnson studied under Li; Mildenhall pioneered NeRF; Lassner pioneered Gaussian Splatting. Together, they represent three of the most important threads in modern 3D AI research. This is a team assembled around a specific technical vision, not a business opportunity.

## Product: Marble

Marble, launched publicly in November 2025 (beta earlier in 2025), is World Labs' first commercial product -- a generative world model that creates navigable, persistent 3D environments from text, images, video, or rough 3D layouts.

Key capabilities:
- **Multi-modal input:** Generate 3D worlds from text prompts, single images, video, or block-out 3D layouts.
- **AI-native editing:** Users can refine generated environments with editing tools, not just prompt-and-pray.
- **Hybrid 3D editor:** Block out spatial structures manually, then let AI fill in visual detail -- a workflow designed for professional creators.
- **Export:** Meshes, video, and other formats for integration into existing 3D pipelines.
- **Persistence:** Generated worlds are navigable and persistent, not just static renders.

The World API (launched January 2026) makes these capabilities available programmatically, enabling integration into third-party tools and workflows.

**Pricing tiers:** Free (4 generations), Standard ($20/mo), Pro ($35/mo, includes commercial rights), Max ($95/mo). API uses credit-based pricing ($1.00 per 1,250 credits).

## What makes them interesting

1. **Fei-Fei Li's singular credibility.** Few founders in AI carry this level of scientific authority. ImageNet literally enabled the deep learning revolution. Her involvement gives World Labs instant legitimacy with researchers, investors, and enterprise partners. The Beijing-to-Stanford immigration narrative also gives the company a compelling founder story that resonates culturally.

2. **The spatial intelligence thesis is contrarian and possibly correct.** Most AI investment has flowed into language and 2D image/video generation. World Labs is betting that 3D spatial understanding is the next critical capability -- for robotics, autonomous vehicles, gaming, architecture, simulation, and eventually general intelligence. If AI needs to interact with the physical world, it needs to understand 3D space. This is a thesis rooted in decades of Fei-Fei Li's research, not a trend-chase.

3. **Technically differentiated founding team.** The four co-founders collectively represent NeRF, Gaussian Splatting, and ImageNet -- three of the most consequential developments in computer vision and 3D AI. This is not a team that will struggle with technical direction.

4. **Autodesk partnership signals enterprise pull.** The $200M strategic investment from Autodesk (the dominant player in 3D design tools) is arguably the most important signal in the company's history. It suggests that World Labs' technology could integrate into existing professional 3D workflows (architecture, film/VFX, game development) rather than needing to build distribution from scratch.

5. **Underexplored market.** While dozens of well-funded startups compete in LLMs and 2D image generation, the "generate and reason about 3D worlds" market has far fewer serious entrants. Competitors exist (Luma AI, Meshy, some capabilities from Runway and Pika), but none with this combination of technical depth, founder prestige, and capital.

6. **Platform potential.** If Marble evolves from a content generation tool into a general-purpose spatial intelligence platform, the TAM expands dramatically -- robotics simulation, digital twins, urban planning, scientific visualization, and beyond.

## Risks and open questions

- **Very early commercially.** Product only launched in November 2025. No public revenue figures. The $5B valuation is based almost entirely on team pedigree, technology promise, and market potential -- not traction.
- **CEO dual role.** Fei-Fei Li maintains her Stanford professorship. While this provides academic credibility and a talent pipeline, it raises legitimate questions about operational bandwidth for a company burning significant capital. Whether she transitions to full-time CEO as the company scales will be worth watching.
- **Market timing uncertainty.** Spatial intelligence is clearly important long-term, but when demand materializes at scale is unclear. Gaming and film/VFX are near-term use cases; robotics and autonomous systems are longer-term.
- **Competition from foundation model labs.** OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Meta are all investing in video and 3D generation. If spatial intelligence becomes clearly valuable, these companies have the resources to compete aggressively.
- **Business model maturity.** The current pricing ($20-$95/mo consumer tiers, credit-based API) is appropriate for early adoption but unclear if it can support the capital intensity of the business at scale. Enterprise contracts (like the Autodesk partnership) will likely need to become the primary revenue driver.

## Notes

- **Confidence on valuation:** The ~$5B Series B valuation comes from Bloomberg reporting and was not officially confirmed by the company. The seed/Series A split ($100M seed + $130M extension) is approximate based on Crunchbase and Contrary Research data.
- **Cisco investment:** Cisco invested separately (announced 2025), amount not publicly disclosed. This is notable as it suggests interest from networking/infrastructure companies in spatial AI, possibly for digital twin or IoT applications.
- **Fei-Fei Li's broader influence:** Beyond World Labs, Li serves on multiple boards and advisory roles. She was elected to the National Academy of Engineering and the National Academy of Medicine. Her policy influence (she has testified before Congress on AI) gives World Labs an unusual degree of access to policymakers.
- **"Spatial intelligence" as a category:** The term is largely World Labs' framing. Whether it becomes an established category (like "foundation models" or "generative AI") or remains a niche descriptor will depend on whether the technology achieves broad adoption.
