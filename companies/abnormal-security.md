# Abnormal AI (formerly Abnormal Security)

## Overview

Abnormal AI is a San Francisco-based cybersecurity company that uses behavioral artificial intelligence to protect enterprises from email-based attacks, including business email compromise (BEC), phishing, ransomware, and account takeover. Founded in 2018 by Evan Reiser and Sanjay Jeyakumar -- who previously built machine learning systems together at TellApart and Twitter -- the company takes a fundamentally different approach to email security: instead of relying on threat signatures, blocklists, or sandboxing, it builds behavioral profiles of every employee and vendor in an organization and flags deviations from normal patterns.

As of March 2026, Abnormal is valued at $5.1 billion, has raised approximately $546 million in total funding, and protects more than 2,000 enterprise customers including 15% of the Fortune 500. The company rebranded from Abnormal Security to Abnormal AI in April 2025, signaling its expansion beyond email into a broader behavioral AI security platform covering Slack, Zoom, Workday, and other cloud applications. An IPO appears to be in preparation, with a former ServiceNow finance executive hired as CFO in early 2025.

## Founding story

The core insight behind Abnormal came from ad tech. Both Reiser and Jeyakumar spent years at TellApart (later acquired by Twitter) building systems that analyzed massive volumes of behavioral data -- purchase patterns, browsing history, engagement signals -- to predict consumer intent and personalize marketing. When they looked at the email security market, they saw an industry still relying on rules-based detection (signature matching, URL scanning) that was fundamentally unable to stop social engineering attacks -- the kind of attacks that look like normal emails but manipulate human behavior.

The founding thesis: if you build a system that understands what "normal" looks like for every person in an organization -- communication patterns, sign-in behavior, tone, typical workflows -- you can detect attacks that have no malicious payload at all. A BEC email from a spoofed CEO asking for a wire transfer contains no malware and no suspicious links; it is only detectable if you know that the CEO doesn't normally email the CFO at 2 AM from an unfamiliar IP requesting urgent payment to a new vendor.

Greylock incubated the company in its San Francisco offices in 2018, with the firm's cybersecurity conviction and the founders' ML pedigree providing a strong foundation. The company publicly launched alongside its $24M Series A in November 2019.

## Funding story

**2018 -- Seed (undisclosed amount).** Greylock Partners incubated the company. Reiser and Jeyakumar developed the core behavioral AI engine during this period.

**2019 -- Series A ($24M).** Led by Greylock. Coincided with the company's public launch. The round funded the initial go-to-market push into enterprise email security.

**2020 -- Series B ($50M).** Led by Menlo Ventures with Greylock participating. This round came during a period of rapid customer acquisition as COVID-19 drove massive increases in both remote work and email-based attacks.

**2022 -- Series C ($210M at $4B valuation).** Led by Insight Partners. A major leap -- the company tripled ARR in the preceding year and crossed into unicorn territory. Greylock and Menlo also participated.

**2024 -- Series D ($250M at $5.1B valuation).** Led by Wellington Management, with Greylock, Menlo, Insight Partners, and CrowdStrike's Falcon Fund participating. Announced alongside the company crossing $200M ARR. A 28% valuation increase from the Series C despite a difficult fundraising climate for most cybersecurity startups.

The investor base is notable for its consistency: Greylock has participated in every known round from seed through Series D. CrowdStrike's strategic investment in the Series D is significant -- it suggests a potential partnership or channel relationship with the largest pure-play cybersecurity company.

## Team

**Founding team profile:** The two co-founders share a distinctive background: both are machine learning engineers who cut their teeth not in security but in ad tech, specifically at TellApart and Twitter. This is unusual in cybersecurity, where most founders come from security operations, penetration testing, or government/intelligence backgrounds. Their outsider perspective is arguably their biggest advantage -- they approached email security as a data science problem rather than a threat intelligence problem.

- **Evan Reiser (CEO):** Serial entrepreneur. Started in ML R&D at Eastman Kodak (2003), then founded Bloomspot (sold to JP Morgan), AdStack (acquired by TellApart), and was part of TellApart's acquisition by Twitter. At Twitter, led product and ML for advertising. RPI engineering background. Reiser is the business-facing leader who sets product direction and drives the go-to-market strategy.

- **Sanjay Jeyakumar (CTO):** Stanford MS in CS, UC Berkeley BS in EECS. Google engineer (2005-2009) working on Search and Android. Founding engineer at TellApart, where he architected the distributed ML systems. At Twitter, he was the systems architect for the ad platform's data processing infrastructure. Jeyakumar is the technical leader who designed Abnormal's behavioral AI engine. Indian-American.

**Key hire:** Lisa Banks joined as CFO in January 2025, coming from ServiceNow where she was SVP of Finance. This is a classic pre-IPO hire -- bringing in a CFO with public-company SaaS experience to prepare financial operations and reporting for a potential listing.

## What makes them interesting

1. **Ad tech DNA applied to security.** The founders' background in behavioral modeling for advertising gives them a genuinely differentiated technical approach. Building per-user behavioral baselines from thousands of signals (communication patterns, authentication events, writing style, typical workflows) is the same problem as building consumer profiles for ad targeting -- just applied to threat detection instead of purchase prediction. This is not how traditional email security companies think.

2. **API-based architecture is a distribution advantage.** Abnormal integrates via API with Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace rather than sitting inline as a mail gateway (like Proofpoint or Mimecast). This means deployment takes minutes, not weeks -- a six-click setup process. For enterprise buyers, this dramatically reduces procurement friction and makes Abnormal easy to layer on top of existing security stacks rather than requiring a rip-and-replace.

3. **Growth metrics are exceptional.** 100% year-over-year ARR growth at $200M+ scale is rare in enterprise SaaS. The company doubled from ~$100M to $200M ARR in roughly 12 months (mid-2023 to mid-2024). If this trajectory has continued, even at a decelerating rate, the company is likely well north of $300M ARR by early 2026 (uncertain -- no public confirmation).

4. **BEC is a massive, underserved market.** Business email compromise caused $2.9 billion in reported losses in 2023 according to the FBI's IC3 report, making it the costliest category of cybercrime. Traditional secure email gateways (SEGs) struggle with BEC because these attacks contain no malicious payloads -- no links, no attachments, just persuasive text. Abnormal's behavioral approach is purpose-built for this gap.

5. **Platform expansion beyond email.** The April 2025 rebrand to Abnormal AI signals serious ambition beyond email security. The company now offers protection across Slack, Zoom, Workday, ServiceNow, and other cloud applications -- applying the same behavioral AI engine to detect anomalous activity across the enterprise collaboration stack. If successful, this expands the TAM significantly from email security (~$5-8B) to broader cloud security.

6. **IPO-ready trajectory.** The CFO hire from ServiceNow, the $5.1B valuation, the $200M+ ARR with triple-digit growth, and the consistent blue-chip investor base all point toward a public listing in the 2026-2027 timeframe. Abnormal is frequently cited in cybersecurity IPO pipeline analyses alongside companies like Wiz and Snyk.

7. **Named a Leader in 2025 Gartner Magic Quadrant for Email Security.** This is a significant credentialing event for enterprise sales -- Gartner MQ placement matters enormously for CISOs making purchasing decisions. Being named a Leader (not just a Niche Player or Visionary) while still private and relatively young is notable.

## Risks and open questions

- **Concentration in email security.** Despite the rebrand and platform expansion, the vast majority of revenue still comes from email. Success in Slack/Zoom/Workday security is unproven at scale.
- **Competition from incumbents.** Microsoft's built-in Defender for Office 365 continues to improve, and Proofpoint (acquired by Thoma Bravo for $12.3B in 2021) and Mimecast are not standing still. The question is whether Abnormal can stay ahead on AI capabilities as incumbents invest heavily in their own ML.
- **Valuation in a crowded cybersecurity IPO window.** Multiple cybersecurity companies are expected to go public in 2026-2027. Market appetite for another $5B+ cybersecurity IPO will depend on the macro environment and public-market comparables.
- **Current ARR figures are uncertain.** The most recent publicly confirmed number is $200M+ as of August 2024. Estimates for 2025-2026 ARR are extrapolations based on stated growth rates.
