# Mind Robotics

## Overview

Mind Robotics is an industrial AI robotics company spun out of Rivian in November 2025 by Rivian founder and CEO RJ Scaringe. Headquartered in Palo Alto, California, the company is building AI-powered robots capable of performing dexterous, variable, and reasoning-intensive tasks in manufacturing environments. In less than five months since its founding, Mind Robotics has raised $615 million in total funding ($115M seed + $500M Series A) at an approximately $2 billion valuation.

The core thesis: existing industrial robots handle repeatable, dimensionally stable tasks well, but a large share of factory value-add work -- parts handling, wiring assembly, component fitting -- requires human-like dexterity, adaptation, and physical reasoning that classical robotics cannot address. Mind Robotics is building the AI foundation (models, hardware, and deployment infrastructure) to close that gap.

## Funding story

**November 2025 -- Seed ($115M).** Led by Eclipse. Hanabi Capital Management also participated. Raised concurrently with the formal spinout from Rivian. Some sources cite the amount as $110M; the discrepancy is unresolved.

**March 2026 -- Series A ($500M at ~$2B valuation).** Co-led by Accel and Andreessen Horowitz (a16z). Accel partner Sameer Gandhi joined the board. Rivian Automotive remains a major shareholder. The round was announced March 11, 2026.

**Total raised: $615M.** This is one of the fastest capitalization trajectories in industrial robotics, driven by investor confidence in the Rivian data advantage and Scaringe's track record as a hardware company builder.

## Team

**RJ Scaringe (Founder & Chairman)** -- Founded Rivian in 2009 and led it from a clean-sheet EV concept through a $12B+ IPO in 2021. PhD in Engineering from the MIT Sloan Automotive Lab. His direct experience building and operating a vertically integrated manufacturing operation at scale is the foundational credential. Scaringe maintains his role as Rivian CEO while serving as chairman of Mind Robotics.

**Broader team origins:** Press coverage describes the founding team as drawing talent from Physical Intelligence, Waymo, Zoox, and Google -- indicating deep expertise in autonomy, perception, reinforcement learning, and real-world robotic system deployment. However, specific names and titles beyond Scaringe have not been publicly disclosed as of March 2026.

**Board:** Sameer Gandhi (Accel partner) joined the board following the Series A.

**Headcount:** Approximately 13 employees as of early 2026 (per LinkedIn), with rapid hiring underway.

## Technology and approach

Mind Robotics takes a deliberately anti-humanoid stance. Scaringe has been vocal: "Doing cartwheels does not create value in manufacturing" (Wall Street Journal, March 2026). Instead of building general-purpose humanoid robots, the company designs robots around specific industrial tasks -- optimizing form factor for the work rather than mimicking the human body.

Key technical elements:

- **AI foundation models** trained on data from Rivian's active, high-volume production lines. The Rivian factories provide a proprietary data flywheel that most robotics startups cannot access.
- **Custom robotic hands and end-effectors** designed for dexterous manipulation in manufacturing contexts (parts handling, wiring, component assembly).
- **Deployment infrastructure** for scaling robot fleets in industrial environments, leveraging Rivian's electro-mechanical engineering expertise.

The focus areas -- dexterous manipulation, object handling, component assembly, and adaptive physical reasoning -- target precisely the factory tasks that remain stubbornly manual because they require the kind of variability and judgment that traditional fixed automation cannot handle.

## Business model: "Captured distribution"

The strategic concept Scaringe calls "captured distribution" is the most distinctive element of Mind Robotics' approach. Rivian serves simultaneously as:

1. **Major shareholder** -- providing financial alignment
2. **Anchor customer** -- guaranteeing a deployment environment at scale
3. **Data source** -- supplying massive, proprietary manufacturing data for model training
4. **Engineering partner** -- contributing electro-mechanical expertise and production knowledge

This structure solves the classic robotics startup bootstrapping problem: you need deployment data to train good models, but you need good models to win deployments. By having Rivian's factories as a captive testing ground, Mind Robotics can iterate rapidly with real production data from day one.

The initial deployment is within Rivian's own factories. Scaringe has stated that Mind Robotics will have "a large number of robots deployed" by end of 2026. The likely path is to prove the platform internally at Rivian, then expand to external manufacturing customers.

Detailed pricing and revenue model have not been publicly disclosed.

## What makes them interesting

1. **The Rivian data moat.** Most AI robotics startups face a cold-start problem: they need real-world manufacturing data but cannot get factory access without a proven product. Mind Robotics starts with a massive, active production environment generating continuous training data. This is a structural advantage that is difficult to replicate.

2. **Scaringe's credibility as a hardware company builder.** Rivian is one of the few successful ground-up automotive companies of the past century. Building and operating EV factories at scale -- with all the supply chain, manufacturing, and engineering complexity that entails -- is directly relevant to deploying industrial robots. This is not a software-only founder learning hardware.

3. **Anti-humanoid contrarianism.** While the robotics hype cycle in 2025-2026 has been dominated by humanoid robots (Figure, Tesla Optimus, 1X, Apptronik), Mind Robotics is explicitly betting against the humanoid form factor for industrial applications. The argument -- that task-specific designs outperform general-purpose human-shaped robots in structured factory environments -- has strong engineering logic, even if it is less visually compelling.

4. **Speed of capitalization.** $615M raised in under five months from founding, at a $2B valuation, with Accel and a16z co-leading the Series A. This reflects extreme investor conviction.

5. **The Physical Intelligence connection.** Team members drawn from Physical Intelligence, Waymo, Zoox, and Google suggest that Mind Robotics has access to frontier knowledge in robot learning, perception, and autonomous systems. The Physical Intelligence lineage is particularly notable given that company's leading position in robot foundation models.

6. **Practical deployment timeline.** Unlike many AI robotics companies that remain in lab or demo mode, Scaringe has committed to large-scale factory deployment by end of 2026. Rivian's factories provide a concrete, measurable venue to prove (or fail to prove) the technology works.

## Key risks and open questions

- **Leadership bandwidth.** Scaringe is simultaneously CEO of Rivian (a public company with its own significant challenges) and chairman of Mind Robotics. The degree to which he can effectively lead both remains unclear, and the operational leadership below him at Mind Robotics has not been publicly identified.
- **Small team, massive ambition.** With approximately 13 employees as of early 2026 and $615M in funding, the company is in a very early stage relative to its capitalization and stated goals.
- **Rivian dependency.** The "captured distribution" advantage is also a concentration risk. If Rivian faces financial difficulties or production slowdowns, Mind Robotics' primary deployment environment and data source could be compromised.
- **Competitive landscape.** Physical Intelligence, Skild AI, and Google DeepMind are all pursuing AI-driven robot manipulation. Incumbent industrial robot companies (FANUC, ABB, KUKA) are also integrating AI capabilities. The window for establishing a dominant position is narrow.
- **Seed round discrepancy.** The seed amount appears as either $110M or $115M across different sources; this is a minor point but reflects the general opacity around the company's early details.
